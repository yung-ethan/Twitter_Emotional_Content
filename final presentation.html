Introduction

Some lifestyle choices unique to the Millennial Generation are habitual attachment to mobile devices and constant streaming of social media. Such a lifestyle shows a drastic change in modes of emotional expression and regard for privacy. To explore this phenomemon, we analyzed the quality and quantity of emotions expressed via tweets. We primarily were interested in Twitter emotion content time trends and the effects of news events, if applicable. We collected Twitter posts from Nov. 25 2013 8p.m to Dec. 3. 2013 9p.m (including Thanksgiving). Our Questions of Interest:
1) What is the general weekly and daily time trend of tweet sentiment?
2) Does tweet emotion content differ on holidays?
3) Are emotional tweets posted on holidays mostly positive or negative?
4) What are the reasons for unexpected changes in emotional trends on Twitter?
5) What is the proportion of positively valenced tweets,negatively valenced tweets, and mixed valenced tweets?
6) Where are tweets coming from and how does the number of hourly tweets change?

  • Methods Results

    • To collect tweets from Twitter, we used streamR in order to scrap local time, timezone, latitude and longitude of tweets as well as emotional content and tweeters’ information.
    Once we acquired the data, we processed tweets for emotionality using a modified function developed for sentiment analysis by Jeffery Breen called "score.sentiment". Based on the MPQA opinion Corpus’ Subjectivity Lexicon, WordNet’s Affect Emotion List, a Lexicon developed by Hu and Liu, and a psychological list of ‘feeling’ words, we defined &lsquosentiment score’ that the number of positive matching words subtracts the number of negative matching words in the given text. Then, we catagoried the emotional contents whether the emotion is positive, negative, or both.

    Confounding Factors and Limitations

    Initially, we set out to investigate how emotions change throughout the course of a day or week. However, we were limited to about a week’s worth of data (which still amounted to many megabytes of information), and the general trends were partially confounded by significant events. For example, Thanksgiving had an overwhelmingly positive effect on emotion, or at least emotion conveyed over Twitter. The other two causes of significant emotional change (Paul Walker’s death, and the signing-on of Jacoby Ellsbury to the Yankees) were surmised by the timing of public news releases, and by reading some of the raw tweet text.
    Additionally, time zone designation was tricky to deal with. The tweets are grouped by their local times, which makes sense for analysis by time of day. However, significant news events will break almost instantaneously across the entire U.S., and thus their emotional impact will be captured across different local times. Additionally, there were some tweets found with impossible local times for tweets in the U.S. (likely because outside time zones were somehow set to those tweets), and thus some tweets in the data are incorrectly timed.

    Conclusion

    The emotional content of Twitter posts was found to follow a regular cyclical pattern throughout the course of day: most negative in the early morning, reaching a peak at around 8 am, decreasing through the afternoon, and increasing again in the late evening. However, emotional content was also highly sensitive to significant events, which confounded our findings. Our process is validated by the fact that the sentiment scores followed these significant events appropriately (e.g., Thanksgiving was overwhelmingly positive). Also, by geographic mapping, we found that tweets cluster around major cities. Further analysis can be done by teasing out the effect of significant events versus cyclical patterns, collecting data for longer periods of time, accounting for events that are instantaneous across time zones, and investigating the differences in emotional content between particular regions. Additionally, the link from emotional content of Twitter posts to peoples’ actual emotions over time should be further examined.
